Corridor 01

Fronto-Temporal

Overview

The fronto-temporal corridor provides access to the anterior and middle cranial fossae through the pterional craniotomy and its extensions. This is the most versatile corridor in neurosurgery, with access to the sylvian fissure, circle of Willis, suprasellar region, anterior clinoid, and the sphenoid wing. The primary workhorse for anterior circulation aneurysms, parasellar and sphenoid wing meningiomas, and lesions involving the sylvian fissure, optic apparatus, and cavernous sinus.

Approaches
Pterional (Frontotemporal Craniotomy)
Classic approach through the sylvian fissure. Access to the circle of Willis, suprasellar cistern, and interpeduncular fossa. Workhorse for anterior circulation aneurysms.
Orbitozygomatic (OZ)
Extends pterional with orbital rim and/or zygomatic arch removal. Reduces brain retraction for deep or high-riding lesions like basilar tip aneurysms and craniopharyngiomas.
Transsylvian
Wide splitting of the sylvian fissure for insular pathology, MCA aneurysms, and mesial temporal lesions accessed laterally.

OR Pearls
Imaging Review

Confirm lesion relationship to the optic apparatus and ICA. Evaluate the sylvian fissure venous drainage pattern, particularly the superficial sylvian vein. For meningiomas, assess vascular encasement and calcification. For aneurysms, evaluate morphology, parent vessel, and relationship to perforators.

Setup

Supine, head in Mayfield, rotated 30-45° contralateral, extended to let frontal lobe fall away from the anterior fossa floor. Malar eminence is the highest point. For OZ extension, plan the skin incision behind the hairline to the zygomatic root. Key early steps: interfascial or subfascial dissection to protect the frontotemporal branch of CN VII, MacCarty keyhole at the frontozygomatic suture.

Decision Points
Pterional vs OZ?
Consider OZ when the corridor to the target is steep or deep, such as basilar tip or large craniopharyngiomas. OZ adds an inferior-to-superior trajectory that reduces retraction.
Sylvian Fissure Split
Inside-out (from the MCA bifurcation) vs outside-in (from the superficial veins). Direction depends on pathology location and venous anatomy.
Anterior Clinoidectomy
Extradural vs intradural removal. Required for paraclinoid aneurysms, clinoidal meningiomas, and optic nerve decompression.
1-Piece vs 2-Piece OZ
One-piece removes orbital rim with craniotomy. Two-piece separates them for more flexibility. Choice depends on exposure needs.
