Corridor 07

Intraventricular

Overview

The intraventricular corridor accesses pathology within the lateral ventricles, third ventricle, and their connections. This corridor is essential for colloid cysts, choroid plexus tumors, subependymomas, central neurocytomas, intraventricular meningiomas, and lesions of the septum pellucidum and fornix. The ventricular system is surrounded by eloquent deep structures — the fornix, internal cerebral veins, thalamostriate veins, and choroidal arteries — making corridor selection and preservation of these structures the central surgical challenge.

Approaches
Transcortical-Transventricular
Cortical incision through the middle frontal gyrus into the lateral ventricle. Direct access to the frontal horn and body of the lateral ventricle. For colloid cysts (transchoroidal or interforniceal), lateral ventricle tumors.
Interhemispheric Transcallosal
Midline interhemispheric approach with callosotomy to enter the lateral ventricle. Avoids cortical incision. For third ventricle lesions via the foramen of Monro, colloid cysts, and midline intraventricular tumors.
Endoscopic Intraventricular
Neuroendoscopy through a burr hole and single cortical trajectory. For colloid cyst aspiration, ETV, biopsy, and small intraventricular tumors. Minimally invasive but limited by single-port access and hemostasis constraints.

OR Pearls
Setup

Supine for all approaches. Transcortical: head neutral or slightly rotated, craniotomy centered over the middle frontal gyrus, corticotomy along a sulcus when possible. Transcallosal: midline craniotomy crossing midline, interhemispheric dissection to the corpus callosum, callosotomy 2–2.5 cm starting at the junction of the anterior and middle third. Navigate to the foramen of Monro using the septal and thalamostriate veins as landmarks.

Decision Points
Transcortical vs Transcallosal?
Transcallosal avoids cortical incision and is preferred for midline/third ventricle lesions. Transcortical is faster and more direct for lateral ventricle tumors, especially in the trigone or temporal horn. If ventriculomegaly is present, transcortical is straightforward.
Foramen of Monro Strategy
Interforniceal approach splits the fornix in the midline (safe if septum is thin). Transchoroidal opens the choroidal fissure along the tenia fornicis. Subchoroidal goes beneath the choroid plexus. Each has different risks to the fornix and internal cerebral veins.
Open vs Endoscopic?
Endoscopic for small lesions, colloid cyst aspiration, ETV, and biopsy. Open microsurgical for large or vascular tumors where hemostasis and bimanual dissection are critical. Hybrid approaches combine endoscopic visualization with microsurgical technique.
Venous Preservation
The thalamostriate vein, internal cerebral veins, and septal veins are landmarks AND critical structures. Sacrifice of the internal cerebral veins risks bilateral thalamic infarction. The thalamostriate vein marks the posterior limit of safe foramen of Monro enlargement.
