Corridor 04

Cerebellopontine Angle

Overview

The cerebellopontine angle corridor accesses the lateral posterior fossa between the cerebellum, pons, and petrous bone. This is the primary route for vestibular schwannomas, CPA meningiomas, epidermoids, and vascular compression syndromes (trigeminal neuralgia, hemifacial spasm). The corridor provides access to cranial nerves V through XI, the vertebrobasilar system, and the lateral brainstem. The relationship between tumor, cranial nerves, and vascular structures defines the surgical strategy.

Approaches
Retrosigmoid
Posterior fossa craniotomy behind the sigmoid sinus. Versatile access to the entire CPA, CN V–XI, and petroclival region. Workhorse for vestibular schwannomas, microvascular decompression, and CPA meningiomas.
Translabyrinthine
Through the mastoid and labyrinth directly into the CPA. Sacrifices hearing but provides wide access to the IAC and facial nerve with minimal cerebellar retraction. For large vestibular schwannomas with non-serviceable hearing.
Far Lateral (Transcondylar Extensions)
Extends the retrosigmoid inferiorly with condyle drilling to access the lower CPA, jugular foramen, and vertebral artery. For jugular foramen schwannomas, lower cranial nerve pathology.

OR Pearls
Setup

Lateral decubitus or park bench for retrosigmoid, supine with head turned for translabyrinthine. Retrosigmoid: head flexed and vertex tilted slightly down to open the CPA. Mastoid emissary vein is the landmark for the transverse-sigmoid junction. Craniotomy exposes the sigmoid sinus anteriorly — this is your anterior limit. Lumbar drain or CSF release from the cisterna magna to relax the cerebellum early.

Decision Points
Retrosigmoid vs Translabyrinthine?
Retrosigmoid preserves hearing (for tumors <2cm with serviceable hearing). Translabyrinthine sacrifices hearing but offers wider IAC access and less cerebellar retraction — preferred for large tumors or non-serviceable hearing.
Facial Nerve Management
Always identify facial nerve before debulking. Stimulation mapping at the brainstem root entry zone and at the porus. The nerve is typically anterior-inferior on the tumor capsule. Internal debulking before capsule dissection.
MVD Target Vessel
Trigeminal neuralgia: SCA or AICA compressing CN V root entry zone. Hemifacial spasm: PICA or AICA on CN VII. Interpose Teflon felt between vessel and nerve — do not just displace the vessel.
Subtotal vs Gross Total
For vestibular schwannomas adherent to the facial nerve, a thin rind of tumor left on the nerve preserves function better than aggressive dissection. Near-total resection with planned observation is often the right call.
