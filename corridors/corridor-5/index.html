Corridor 05

Midline Posterior Fossa

Overview

The midline posterior fossa corridor encompasses inferior and superior suboccipital approaches providing access to the cerebellar vermis, fourth ventricle, dorsal brainstem, and pineal region. This is the primary route for midline cerebellar tumors (medulloblastoma, pilocytic astrocytoma, hemangioblastoma), fourth ventricle ependymomas, pineal region tumors, and Chiari malformations. The corridor spans from the torcula superiorly to the foramen magnum inferiorly, with the cerebellar hemispheres defining the lateral boundaries.

Approaches
Midline Suboccipital (Telovelar)
Posterior fossa craniotomy with access to the fourth ventricle through the cerebellomedullary fissure, avoiding vermian splitting. For fourth ventricle tumors and dorsal brainstem pathology.
Supracerebellar Infratentorial
Access to the pineal region by working above the cerebellum and below the tentorium. For pineal tumors, tectal lesions, and posterior third ventricle pathology.
Suboccipital Decompression
Removal of the posterior arch of C1 and suboccipital bone with duraplasty (with or without tonsillar reduction depending on anatomy). For Chiari I malformation with syringomyelia.

OR Pearls
Imaging Review

Hydrocephalus status and need for preoperative CSF diversion. Tumor relationship to the fourth ventricle floor, including degree of brainstem invasion versus displacement. Enhancement pattern to help narrow the differential. For pineal region tumors, relationship to the vein of Galen and deep venous system. Whether the tumor extends through the foramina of Magendie or Luschka.

Setup

Prone for midline suboccipital, sitting or prone for supracerebellar infratentorial. Midline linear skin incision, subperiosteal dissection to the foramen magnum, suboccipital craniectomy or craniotomy below the transverse sinuses. Open cisterna magna early for CSF drainage and cerebellar relaxation. For telovelar approach, separate the uvula and tonsils to expose the tela choroidea and inferior medullary velum without splitting the vermis.

Decision Points
Telovelar vs Transvermian?
Telovelar through the cerebellomedullary fissure preserves the vermis and reduces the risk of cerebellar mutism, particularly in pediatric patients. Transvermian only when the vermis is already destroyed or displaced by tumor.
Sitting vs Prone?
Sitting position for supracerebellar infratentorial gives gravity-assisted cerebellar retraction but carries risk of venous air embolism. Prone with head flexed is safer. Patient factors and surgeon experience determine the choice.
EVD Timing
Pre-resection EVD for obstructive hydrocephalus vs proceeding directly to tumor resection. Aggressive CSF drainage before resection carries a risk of upward herniation.
Fourth Ventricle Floor
Tumors adherent to the floor are typically left with a thin residual to avoid brainstem injury. Identify the safe zones on the floor (e.g., suprafacial and infrafacial triangles) before attempting any dissection.
