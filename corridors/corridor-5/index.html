Corridor 05

Midline Posterior Fossa

Overview

The midline posterior fossa corridor provides access to the cerebellar vermis, fourth ventricle, pineal region, and dorsal brainstem through suboccipital approaches. This is the primary route for midline cerebellar tumors (medulloblastoma, pilocytic astrocytoma, hemangioblastoma), fourth ventricle ependymomas, pineal region tumors, and Chiari malformations. The corridor spans from the torcula superiorly to the foramen magnum inferiorly, with the cerebellar hemispheres defining the lateral boundaries.

Approaches
Midline Suboccipital (Telovelar)
Midline posterior fossa craniotomy with telovelar approach to the fourth ventricle through the cerebellomedullary fissure. Avoids vermian splitting. For fourth ventricle tumors, vermian lesions, and dorsal brainstem pathology.
Supracerebellar Infratentorial
Access to the pineal region by working above the cerebellum and below the tentorium. Sitting or prone position. For pineal tumors, tectal lesions, and posterior third ventricle pathology.
Suboccipital Decompression
Removal of the posterior arch of C1 and suboccipital bone with duraplasty. For Chiari I malformation with syringomyelia. May include tonsillar reduction or arachnoid dissection.

OR Pearls
Setup

Prone or concorde position for midline suboccipital, sitting or prone for supracerebellar infratentorial. Midline: linear skin incision, subperiosteal dissection to the foramen magnum, craniotomy below the transverse sinuses. Open cisterna magna early for CSF drainage and cerebellar relaxation. For telovelar approach, the uvula and tonsils are separated to expose the tela choroidea and inferior medullary velum without splitting the vermis.

Decision Points
Telovelar vs Transvermian?
Telovelar through the cerebellomedullary fissure preserves the vermis and avoids cerebellar mutism. Transvermian only for large tumors where the vermis is already destroyed or displaced. Telovelar is almost always preferred.
Sitting vs Prone?
Sitting position for supracerebellar infratentorial gives gravity-assisted cerebellar retraction but carries risk of venous air embolism. Prone with head flexed is safer. Lateral decubitus is a compromise. Precordial Doppler and central line mandatory for sitting.
EVD Timing
Pre-resection EVD for obstructive hydrocephalus from fourth ventricle tumors. Some surgeons proceed directly to resection and CSF diversion resolves. Risk of upward herniation with aggressive drainage pre-resection.
Floor of the Fourth Ventricle
Never violate the floor — this is the dorsal brainstem. Tumors adherent to the floor get near-total resection with a thin rind left. Map the facial colliculus and striae medullaris as landmarks for safe zones.
