Corridor 06

Craniovertebral Junction

Overview

The craniovertebral junction corridor accesses the foramen magnum, lower clivus, anterior brainstem at the cervicomedullary junction, and the C1-C2 complex. This corridor is essential for foramen magnum meningiomas, ventral brainstem lesions, vertebral artery aneurysms, and CVJ instability requiring fixation. The vertebral arteries, lower cranial nerves (IX–XII), and the cervicomedullary junction define the critical neurovascular anatomy. Understanding the bony anatomy of the occipital condyles, C1 lateral masses, and C2 pars is fundamental to both tumor surgery and instrumented fusion.

Approaches
Far Lateral (Transcondylar)
Lateral suboccipital craniotomy with partial condyle resection to access the ventrolateral foramen magnum and lower clivus. For foramen magnum meningiomas, vertebral artery aneurysms, and ventral cervicomedullary lesions.
Posterior Midline (Foramen Magnum Decompression)
Suboccipital craniectomy with C1 laminectomy for dorsal pathology and Chiari malformation. Extended inferiorly for upper cervical intradural tumors. Can include occipitocervical fusion if instability present.
Endoscopic Endonasal (Transclival-Transodontoid)
Ventral access to the lower clivus and odontoid through the nose. For ventral cervicomedullary compression from pannus, os odontoideum, or chordomas extending to the CVJ. Requires occipitocervical fusion if destabilizing.

OR Pearls
Setup

Park bench or lateral decubitus for far lateral, prone for posterior midline. Far lateral: hockey-stick incision curving from behind the mastoid to the upper cervical midline. Subperiosteal dissection exposes the suboccipital triangle — the vertebral artery lives in the triangle bounded by the superior oblique, inferior oblique, and rectus capitis posterior major. Identify and mobilize V3 segment of the vertebral artery before any condyle drilling.

Decision Points
How Much Condyle?
Posterior third of the condyle can be removed without destabilizing. Beyond 50% risks occipitocervical instability requiring fusion. Most foramen magnum meningiomas need minimal condyle removal — the exposure comes from lateral positioning and dural opening, not bone.
Vertebral Artery Control
Must identify V3 segment in the suboccipital triangle before opening dura. If tumor encases the intradural vertebral artery, proximal and distal control before dissection. Consider preop balloon test occlusion for tumors encasing a dominant vertebral.
Fusion Decision
Occipitocervical fusion if condyle resection >50%, if CVJ ligaments are incompetent, or if ventral decompression (transodontoid) removes the dens. Occiput-C2 or occiput-C3 constructs with lateral mass or pars screws.
Lower Cranial Nerve Preservation
CN IX–XII course through the jugular foramen and hypoglossal canal at the edges of this corridor. Foramen magnum meningiomas displace these nerves superolaterally. Identify early and work medial to them when possible.
