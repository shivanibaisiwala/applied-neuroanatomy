Corridor 08

Transorbital & Orbital

Overview

The transorbital and orbital corridor accesses the orbit, orbital apex, optic canal, superior orbital fissure, and anterior cranial fossa through or around the orbital walls. This corridor is essential for orbital tumors (cavernous hemangiomas, schwannomas, dermoids, lymphomas), optic nerve sheath meningiomas, sphenoid wing meningiomas with orbital extension, and orbital decompression for thyroid eye disease. Endoscopic transorbital approaches are an emerging access route to the lateral middle cranial fossa and anterior skull base through the orbit as a natural corridor.

Approaches
Lateral Orbitotomy (Krönlein)
Lateral wall removal for intraconal and extraconal lateral orbital lesions. Standard approach for cavernous hemangiomas and lacrimal gland tumors. Can be combined with craniotomy for lesions extending intracranially.
Endoscopic Transorbital
Emerging approach using the orbit as a corridor to the middle cranial fossa, lateral cavernous sinus, and Meckel's cave. Through an eyelid incision and lateral orbital wall. For middle fossa lesions not accessible endonasally.
Transcranial (with Orbital Osteotomy)
Frontotemporal craniotomy with orbital roof or rim removal for lesions spanning the orbit and anterior/middle fossa. For sphenoid wing meningiomas with orbital invasion, optic canal decompression, and orbital apex tumors.

OR Pearls
Setup

Supine, head neutral or slightly extended. Lateral orbitotomy: lateral canthotomy incision, dissection to the lateral orbital rim, rim osteotomy with reciprocating saw or craniotome. Periorbita opened to access the intraconal or extraconal space. For transcranial approaches with orbital osteotomy, plan the craniotomy to include the orbital roof and connect to the superior orbital fissure for orbital apex access.

Decision Points
Lateral vs Superior vs Endoscopic?
Lateral orbitotomy for lateral and intraconal lesions. Superior (transcranial with orbital roof removal) for lesions extending intracranially or at the orbital apex. Endoscopic transorbital for middle fossa access. Medial orbitotomy (endonasal) for medial orbital lesions.
Optic Canal Decompression
Unroof the optic canal by drilling the orbital roof medially over the canal. Open the falciform ligament and optic sheath. Extradural approach from a frontotemporal craniotomy or endoscopic endonasal from medial. Beware the ophthalmic artery entering inferolaterally.
Intraconal Access
The muscle cone defines the surgical corridors within the orbit. Work between the rectus muscles to reach intraconal lesions. Lateral approach between the lateral rectus and superior rectus is the most common. Preserve the ciliary nerves and vessels along the muscle cone walls.
Globe Protection
Corneal shield throughout the case. Intermittent monitoring of intraocular pressure during retraction. Lateral canthotomy provides additional exposure and reduces globe pressure. Post-op check for proptosis, vision, and pupil function immediately upon awakening.
